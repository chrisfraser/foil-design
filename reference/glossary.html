Hydrodynamic Foils · Reference

Glossary

The shared vocabulary for the course. Every lesson uses these terms exactly as defined here.

Orientation
A depressor is just a wing turned over so its “lift” points down. So most of this glossary is ordinary wing/foil language — only the direction of the useful force is flipped.

The device

Foil
A shaped surface (a wing, blade, or plate) that turns flowing fluid to generate a force mostly perpendicular to the flow. “Hydrofoil” = a foil working in water.
Depressor / Diving planer
A towed foil that produces downward force to pull a fishing lure or line deep while trolling. Same physics as a wing, oriented for downforce.
Paravane
A towed “water kite” that planes sideways or downward off a line — the broad family depressors belong to.
Otter board / Trawl door
Commercial-fishing foils that spread or sink a trawl net. The best-documented engineering cousins of a depressor (low aspect ratio, often cambered).

Geometry of a foil

Chord c
Straight-line distance from the leading edge to the trailing edge — the “depth” of the foil in the flow direction.
Span b
Tip-to-tip width, measured across the flow.
Planform area A (or S)
The footprint area seen from above — roughly span × chord. The area used in the lift/drag equations.
Aspect ratio AR = b² / A
How long-and-skinny vs short-and-stubby the foil is. Gliders: high AR. Depressors: low AR — which leaks lift and adds drag (covered later).
Camber
The curvature of the foil’s mean line. A cambered (asymmetric) section makes force even at zero angle of attack — useful for a depressor.
Thickness
How fat the section is, as a % of chord. Affects strength, drag, and stall behaviour.
NACA section
A standardised airfoil shape. e.g. NACA 2412 = 2% camber, located at 40% chord, 12% thick. We’ll generate these in a tool.

Forces & angles

Angle of attack α (alpha)
The angle between the foil’s chord line and the oncoming flow. The primary control knob for how much force the foil makes.
Lift L
The force component perpendicular to the oncoming flow. For a depressor, we aim this downward. (We still call it “lift” — it’s the technical name for the perpendicular force.)
Drag D
The force component along the flow — resistance you must tow against.
Dynamic pressure q = ½ ρ v²
The “pressure of motion” — how hard the moving water pushes. Scales with the square of speed, so doubling speed quadruples the force.
Stall
When angle of attack gets too steep, flow separates from the foil, lift collapses and drag spikes. There’s a max useful α.

Coefficients & performance

Lift coefficient CL
A dimensionless number capturing how effective the foil shape + angle are at making lift, independent of size/speed/fluid.
Drag coefficient CD
Same idea for drag.
Lift-to-drag ratio L/D
Efficiency: downforce gained per unit of towing drag paid. Higher is better.
Polar
A plot of CL vs CD (or vs α) for a section. The chart you read to choose an operating angle.
Induced drag
Extra drag created as a by-product of making lift on a finite wing — worse at low aspect ratio.

Operating in water

Reynolds number Re = ρ v L / μ
Ratio of inertial to viscous forces; sets whether flow is smooth (laminar) or turbulent. You must match your foil’s Re when reading polars from tools.
Cavitation
At high speed/low pressure, water boils into vapour bubbles that wreck lift and erode the surface. A concern toward the 8–10 kn top end.
Cavitation number σ (sigma)
A dimensionless measure of how close the flow is to cavitating. Lower σ = more likely to cavitate.
