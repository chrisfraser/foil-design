Verification & Trust

A CFD result isn't a fact — it's a claim. This lesson is how you make it earn your belief before you cut metal (or filament).

⏱ ~13 minutes• 🧮 Reading convergence, no calculus• The skill that separates real results from pretty pictures
◎ Why this lesson

In Lesson 10 you got a number out of SimScale. Here's the uncomfortable truth: a CFD solver will always give you a confident, beautifully-coloured number — even when it's badly wrong. The whole value of CFD collapses if you can't tell a trustworthy result from a plausible fiction. This lesson is the discipline that earns trust: mesh-independence, y+, convergence, and validation against a known answer.

1Three different words people muddle

“It converged” gets thrown around as if it meant “it's right.” It doesn't. Keep these straight:

  • Converged — the solver's residuals have settled and the forces stopped wandering for this mesh. Necessary, but the weakest bar.
  • Mesh-independent — refine the mesh and the answer stops changing. Now you're reading the equation's answer, not your grid's.
  • Validated — your setup reproduces a known, real answer (experiment or trusted data) for a similar case. Only now does the number describe the world.

A result can be perfectly converged, on a coarse mesh, validated against nothing — and completely wrong. All three gates, every time.

2Mesh-independence: refine until it stops moving

A mesh that's too coarse can't resolve the steep pressure and velocity changes near the foil, so it systematically over-reads the drag. The fix isn't to guess — it's to run the same case at two or three refinements and watch the coefficient settle onto a plateau. The plateau value is your answer; the gap between coarse and plateau is your error.

Drag the refinement slider. Watch the drag fall toward the grid-independent line and the error shrink — and notice that the first few levels lie to you by tens of percent.

Qualitative model of a grid-refinement study. Real practice: at least three meshes, and you stop when the change between the two finest is small (a percent or two) compared to the precision you need.

3y+ : are your wall cells in the right place?

Drag and stall are born in the boundary layer, the thin sheared film on the foil surface. Whether your mesh resolves it is measured by y+, a dimensionless wall distance for the first cell. The rule of thumb: y+ ≈ 1 to resolve the layer directly (or a controlled higher range if you lean on wall functions). A y+ of 50 with a model that wanted 1 means your drag is wrong no matter how converged the run looks — that's why the calculator above flags it.

4Validation: reproduce a known answer first

Before you trust CFD on your novel depressor — which has no “right answer” to check against — prove your whole setup on a case that does. The classic is the NACA 0012 section, measured to death in wind tunnels and curated by NASA's Turbulence Modeling Resource.1 Run it with your mesh strategy, turbulence model and y+, and check you land on the published lift and drag. If you can't reproduce a known foil, you have no business believing your unknown one.

★ The one win from this lesson

Converged ≠ mesh-independent ≠ validated. Earn trust in this order: drive residuals down (converged), refine the mesh until the answer stops moving (mesh-independent) with y+ ≈ 1, then reproduce a known case (validated). And always sanity-check against your hand calc from Lessons 2–7. A number that fails any gate is decoration, not data.

What you can now say

  1. Converged (residuals settled) ≠ mesh-independent (answer stops changing) ≠ validated (matches a known case).
  2. Coarse meshes over-read drag; refine until the coefficient plateaus.
  3. Target y+ ≈ 1 so the boundary layer is resolved.
  4. Validate your method on NACA 0012 before trusting your own foil — and sanity-check every result by hand.
