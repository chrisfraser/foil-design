Hydrodynamic Foils · Lesson 01

A Depressor Is an Upside-Down Wing

The one idea the whole course hangs from: a foil makes a force across the flow — point that force down, and it dives.

Your depressor and an aeroplane wing are the same machine. A wing pushes air down and gets shoved up. Turn it over, tow it through water, and it pushes water up and gets shoved down — dragging your lure deep. Everything else you'll learn is just how much force, and at what cost.

The two forces a foil makes

When water flows past an angled foil, the foil bends that flow. By Newton's third law, the redirected water pushes back on the foil. We split that single push into two components, defined relative to the oncoming flow:

  • Lift — the part perpendicular to the flow. This is the useful force. (Yes, it's still called "lift" even when we aim it downward.1)
  • Drag — the part along the flow. This is the toll: the force you must tow against.

The control knob: angle of attack

How much force? The biggest lever you control is the angle of attack, α — the angle between the foil's chord line and the oncoming flow.2 Tilt the foil more into the flow and (up to a point) it makes more lift. Tilt too far and the flow can't follow the surface any more: it separates, lift collapses, drag spikes. That breakdown is stall, and it sets the maximum useful angle.

Hand-out-the-car-window
Flat palm, level: almost no vertical force — just drag. Tilt your hand a few degrees and it gets shoved up or down hard. Tilt it to 60° and it just batters backward — that's stall. Your hand is a (bad, flat) foil, and α is the only thing you changed.

Play with it

Drag the angle of attack slider and watch the two force arrows respond: lift stays perpendicular to the flow, drag stays along it. Push the angle past about 14° and the foil stalls — lift collapses, the wake turns turbulent. Then hit Flip to wing / depressor and notice the only thing that changes is which way the perpendicular force points.

Try to answer before you slide: at what angle is lift biggest? What happens to drag as you approach stall? (This is a model for intuition — exact numbers come next lesson.)

Where this is heading (the lift equation)

The amount of lift is captured by one equation we'll live inside for the next few lessons. Don't memorise it yet — just meet it:

L = ½ · ρ · v² · A · CL L force  ·  ρ water density  ·  v speed  ·  A foil area  ·  CL lift coefficient (shape & angle)

Read it as a story: force grows with how dense the fluid is (water is ~800× denser than air — why a tiny foil works underwater), with speed squared (double the trolling speed → 4× the force), with area, and with a shape-and-angle number CL that we'll learn to look up and tune.3

The one win from this lesson
A foil makes a force perpendicular to the flow (lift) plus a toll along the flow (drag). A depressor aims the perpendicular force down. The main knob is the angle of attack; push it too far and the foil stalls.
