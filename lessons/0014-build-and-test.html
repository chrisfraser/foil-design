Phase 3 · Build & Test · Lesson 14

Build & On-Water Test

Every number you've computed has been a prediction. The water is where they get graded — and where you finally close the loop.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 One ratio, measured not modelled• The finale: prediction meets reality
◎ Why this lesson

You've come a long way: you understand how a foil makes force, you can compute its L/D by hand, you can run it through CFD and tell a trustworthy number from a pretty one (Lesson 11), and you've sized and printed a real part (Lesson 12, Lesson 13). Now comes the only test that counts. This lesson takes your design to the water, teaches a measurement-first protocol, and shows you how to back out your rig's real-world factor η — the single number that makes your Lesson 3 depth model finally tell the truth. Then you reconcile hand-calc vs CFD vs reality, and you iterate. That's the whole course, closed into a loop you own.

1Print & rig — the last mile before the water

Lesson 12 covered strength and printing in depth, so this is brief. Three things turn a printed shape into a towable depressor:

  • Print orientation & material. Use the orientation and material you settled on in Lesson 12 — layers running so the tow load works across them, not peeling them apart. A tough filament (PETG / nylon / ASA) survives impacts and water far better than brittle PLA.
  • Smooth and seal the surface. Layer lines are roughness, and roughness trips the boundary layer and adds drag at the low Reynolds numbers a small foil runs at. Sand the working faces and seal the print (epoxy or primer) so it doesn't soak up water and so the section you designed is the section that flies.
  • A solid tow point. Fit a real bolt or swivel where your design put the tow eye — captured in the print or through a metal insert, not a glued tab. Put it at the spot your geometry expects; moving the tow point changes the effective angle of attack.

If your design has an adjustable angle, treat it as a lever. A steeper angle raises CL (more downforce) but usually lowers L/D once you push past the polar's sweet spot (Lesson 4) — and from Lesson 3, lower L/D means a shallower line angle. So on the water you have exactly two practical depth knobs: the foil's angle (which sets L/D) and the line length you let out. Hold one fixed while you change the other, or you'll never know which did what.

2A measurement-first test protocol

The cardinal rule of this whole lesson: you cannot improve what you don't measure. A test that produces a feeling (“seemed deep enough”) produces nothing. A test that produces numbers feeds straight back into your model. Run it like this:

  1. Mark the line at known intervals. Paint or tape the towline every 5 m, or clip on a cheap depth/temperature logger. Without a depth reading you have no data — just vibes.
  2. Tow at a steady, known speed. Use GPS speed-over-ground and hold it. Let out a known length of line. Change one variable at a time — this is an experiment, not a fishing trip.
  3. Record speed, line out, and depth for several combinations: a few speeds × a few line lengths. A small table of (v, ℓ, depth) is the entire payload of the day.
  4. Watch for problems while you tow: porpoising or wandering (instability); the foil planing up to the surface (too little downforce or too much line drag); vibration or buzzing (possible cavitation, Lesson 12); or the rig pulling alarmingly hard (tow load — remember from Lesson 2 it grows with v², not depth).

Tip: recover the foil and inspect it between runs. Surface pitting or a chalky etched patch is the fingerprint of cavitation; a bent tow point is a tow-load warning.

3Close the loop — calibrate η

Now the payoff. Your Lesson 3 model says the ideal depth is ℓ·sin θ, where θ = arctan(L/D) from your foil's lift-to-drag ratio. Reality always falls short of that ceiling because of line drag and net weight. The ratio of what you measured to what the model promised is your rig's real-world factor — the single number that bundles every loss together:

η = depthmeasured / (ℓ · sin θ) θ = arctan(L/D) · η typically lands around 0.5–0.8 for a real towed rig

This is the same real-world factor slider you met back in Lesson 3 — except now you're not guessing it, you're measuring it. Plug in your foil's L/D, the line you let out, and the depth you actually recorded, and read off η. Then feed that η back into the Lesson 3 model and every future depth prediction for your rig becomes honest.

5Calibrate trust in the tools

You now hold three estimates of the same foil, made completely differently:

  • your hand calc — the lift equation and force balance from Lessons 2–4;
  • your CFD result — the verified, mesh-independent number from Phase 2; and
  • reality — what the water just told you.

Lay them side by side. Where they agree, trust the toolchain — you can now predict a foil before you ever build it. Where they diverge, you've learned something specific about your model: a hand-calc that over-predicts depth is usually missing induced drag (finite span, Lesson 6) or low-Re losses; a model that beats reality is almost always forgetting line drag (which is exactly what η absorbs). This reconciliation — three independent numbers, forced to face each other — is the scientific payoff of the entire course.

6Iterate — the loop doesn't end

You now own a repeatable design cycle. Change one thing — section, area, aspect ratio, angle, or line — re-predict with your now-calibrated model, re-print if needed, and re-test. Each turn tightens the gap between what you predict and what you get. That's not a footnote to the course; it is the course:

understand → compute → print → test → iterate ↺

★ The one win from this lesson

The water is the only judge that matters. Measure depth against speed and line length, back out your rig's real-world factor η = measured / (ℓ · sin θ), and feed it into the Lesson 3 model so predictions go honest. Then reconcile hand-calc vs CFD vs reality — agreement earns trust, disagreement teaches you (usually induced drag, low-Re, or line drag). You now have a closed design loop: understand → compute → print → test → iterate.

🏁 You've closed the loop

That's the whole course. You can now design a hydrodynamic foil from first principles, compute its forces by hand and verify them in CFD, print a part that survives the tow, and test it in a way that actually tells you something. Most importantly, you can close the gap between prediction and reality and keep closing it. The loop is yours now — every foil you build will be a little truer than the last. Go turn it. 🎣
