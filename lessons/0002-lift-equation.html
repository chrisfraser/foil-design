Hydrodynamic Foils · Lesson 02

The Lift Equation, With Numbers

From “a foil makes a force” to “this foil makes 6.9 kg of downforce at 5 knots.”

In Lesson 1 you met the lift equation. Now we make it pay: plug in your speed, your foil size, and a shape number, and read out how many kilograms of pull your depressor produces. This is the calculation you'll use to size every foil you print.

First: dynamic pressure, ½ρv²

Before lift, one building block. Moving water carries a “pressure of motion” called dynamic pressure:1

q = ½ · ρ · v² ρ (rho) = water density · v = speed  |  result in pascals (Pa = N/m²)

Two things to burn in:

  • Water is dense. ρ ≈ 1025 kg/m³ in seawater (1000 in fresh) — about 800× air. That single factor is why a foil the size of your hand can haul tackle down dozens of metres.2
  • Speed is squared. The v² means force is brutally speed-sensitive: go from 5 to 10 knots and dynamic pressure — and every force — quadruples.
Work in metres-per-second
The equation needs SI units. Convert knots first: 1 knot = 0.514 m/s. So 5 kn ≈ 2.57 m/s, and your 3–10 kn range is ≈ 1.54–5.14 m/s.

The lift equation

Lift is just dynamic pressure acting over the foil's area, scaled by a shape-and-angle number:

L = q · A · CL = ½ · ρ · v² · A · CL A = planform area (m²) · CL = lift coefficient (dimensionless)

A you control with a ruler. CL is the one you can't eyeball — it bundles the foil's section shape and angle of attack into a single number, typically 0.3–1.2 for a working foil before stall. Getting an honest CL for your shape is exactly what the computational tools (XFLR5, SimScale) are for — that's a later lesson. For now we'll assume a value to feel the equation.

A worked depressor

Say a plate-style depressor, 20 cm × 10 cm (so A = 0.020 m²), running at a moderate angle giving CL = 1.0, towed at 5 knots in seawater:

L = ½ · 1025 · 2.57² · 0.020 · 1.0 ≈ 68 N ≈ 6.9 kgf

Nearly seven kilograms of downward pull from a hand-sized plate. Now push it to 10 knots and — because of v² — it leaps to ≈ 271 N ≈ 27.6 kgf. Same foil, four times the force.

Try it — size your own foil

Slide speed, area, and the coefficients. Watch the equation rebuild itself, and feel how speed dominates. Aim for a downforce that makes sense for your tackle, at the speed you actually troll.

The numbers are only as good as CL/CD — placeholders until you measure them in a tool. The point now is the shape of the relationship.

Drag is the same equation

Swap CL for a drag coefficient CD and you get the tow load — the force your line and rod must hold:

D = ½ · ρ · v² · A · CD

CD is much smaller than CL for a good foil. Their ratio L/D (in the calculator) is the efficiency: downforce won per unit of drag paid. A high L/D means deep dive for little tow load — the whole game when you design the section later.

The one win from this lesson
Downforce is L = ½ρv²·A·CL. Density makes water foils punchy; speed is squared (2× speed → 4× force); area is your ruler; CL is the shape-and-angle number you'll get from a tool. Drag is the same formula with CD, and L/D is efficiency.
