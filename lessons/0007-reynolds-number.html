Reynolds Number in Water

The polars from the last few lessons are only true at one speed-and-size — and a single number tells you which polar to trust for your foil.

⏱ ~13 minutes• 🧮 One formula, one log scale• The number that picks your polar
◎ Why this lesson

Lesson 4 taught you to read a polar, and Lessons 5–6 showed what shapes it. But every polar carries a hidden footnote: “valid at this Reynolds number only.” Pick a polar measured at the wrong Re and it will quietly lie to you — promising less drag and more lift than your small, slow depressor can ever deliver. This lesson hands you the one number that matches a polar to your conditions, so the curves you read are the curves you'll actually get.

1What the Reynolds number actually is

The Reynolds number is a single dimensionless ratio: the strength of inertial forces (the flow's momentum, wanting to barrel straight on) against viscous forces (the fluid's internal stickiness, wanting to smooth everything out).1 High Re means inertia wins and viscosity is a thin afterthought; low Re means stickiness dominates the flow near the surface.

Re = v · L / ν = ρ · v · L / μ v speed  ·  L length scale — use the foil chord  ·  ν kinematic viscosity  ·  ρ density  ·  μ dynamic viscosity (ν = μ/ρ)

For water the kinematic viscosity is ν ≈ 1.0×10⁻⁶ m²/s at about 20 °C, rising to ≈ 1.3×10⁻⁶ m²/s in colder ~10 °C water.2 Because Re has no units, it lets you compare a fish-sized foil and a ship's rudder on the same scale — what matters is the combination of speed, size and fluid, not any one of them alone.

2Why it governs the boundary layer — and your drag

Re matters because it controls the thin film of slowed water hugging the foil: the boundary layer. It decides whether that layer stays laminar (smooth, low-friction, but fragile) or trips turbulent (messier but stickier to the surface), and — crucially — when it separates and triggers stall.

At low Re the boundary layer is thick, sluggish, and prone to letting go early, often forming a laminar separation bubble that fattens the wake.3 The penalty is blunt: higher drag and a lower CL,max than the glossy textbook polars promise. So a polar measured at Re = 3×10⁶ is simply the wrong document if your foil runs at Re = 2×10⁵ — same shape, different animal.

3A worked number for a real depressor

Take a typical small depressor: a 100 mm chord towed at 5 knots (≈ 2.57 m/s) through 20 °C water. Drop it into the formula:

Re = 2.57 · 0.1 / 1.0×10⁻⁶ ≈ 2.6×10⁵ v = 2.57 m/s  ·  L = 0.1 m  ·  ν = 1.0×10⁻⁶ m²/s

That ≈ 2.6×10⁵ sits squarely in the low-Re foil regime (~1×10⁵–5×10⁵) — exactly the awkward band where real foils underperform their high-Re polars. This is not an edge case; it's where most trolling depressors live.

4Why water isn't air at the “same” speed

People assume water, being denser and stickier, must put you at a higher Re than air. The opposite per-unit-speed: air's kinematic viscosity (ν ≈ 1.5×10⁻⁵ m²/s) is about 15× larger than water's, so for the same v and L, water gives roughly 15× the Reynolds number air would. Good news — except our depressors are so small and slow that even with that 15× boost we're still down in the low-Re band where care is needed. The lesson isn't “water is high-Re”; it's always match the polar's Re to your own Re, whatever fluid you're in.

5Explore where your foil lands

Set the calculator to your trolling speed and your chord, then toggle the water temperature and watch Re slide along the log scale. Find which band you land in — most small, slow depressors come to rest in the low-Re foil regime (~1×10⁵–5×10⁵), the band where high-Re polars over-predict performance. Push the chord or speed up and watch the marker march toward the better-behaved bands.

A bigger chord or a faster tow buys you a higher Re and generally cleaner, lower-drag flow — one more reason size and speed quietly help. But you can't always have them, so the practical move is to read polars at the Re you've got.

★ The one win from this lesson

Re = v·L/ν decides which polar applies. Small, slow depressors live at low Re (~10⁵–10⁵·⁵), where real drag is higher and CL,max lower than textbook high-Re polars promise. Always match the polar's Reynolds number to your own.

What you can now say

  1. Re = v·L/ν is the ratio of inertial to viscous forces — a dimensionless flow-similarity number.
  2. Re governs the boundary layer: low Re → thick, early-separating layer → higher drag, lower CL,max.
  3. A polar is valid at one Re only; always match it to your conditions.
  4. A 100 mm foil at 5 kn ≈ 2.6×10⁵ — the low-Re regime where most depressors live.
  5. Water gives ~15× air's Re at equal v and L, but small slow foils are still low-Re.
