Coefficients, Polars & Stall

Where the magic number L/D actually comes from — and why your best efficiency hides at a modest angle, not the steepest one.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 Reading graphs, no calculus• The shape of every foil's character
◎ Why this lesson

Lesson 3 showed your dive depth rides on one number: the foil's L/D. But where does it come from? Every foil's whole personality — how much lift it makes at each angle, how much drag it pays, when it gives up — lives in two dimensionless numbers, CL and CD, and the curves they trace. Learn to read those curves and you can judge any section before you ever cut or print it.

1Coefficients strip away everything but shape and angle

Recall the lift equation, L = ½ρv²·A·CL. Speed, density and area are bookkeeping — you set them with the throttle and a ruler. Everything about the foil itself is bottled in CL, the lift coefficient: a pure number, the same in air or water, at 3 knots or 30, that says “how good is this shape, at this angle, at making lift.” Drag gets its own, CD. Strip away the conditions and a foil is its CL and CD.

2CL rises with angle — until it doesn't

Tilt a foil into the flow and CL climbs almost in a straight line with angle of attack — for a thin foil, roughly 0.1 per degree.1 Two features of that line matter:

  • The zero-lift angle: a symmetric foil makes zero lift at α = 0°, but a cambered one already makes lift at 0° — its line is shifted left, crossing zero at a negative angle. (More on camber next lesson.)
  • CL,max and stall: the line can't rise forever. Past a critical angle the flow separates, lift collapses, and drag spikes — stall, the same breakdown you drove in Lesson 1.

3The drag polar and the “bucket”

Plot CL against CD instead and you get the polar — the single most useful chart in foil design.2 A good section has a drag bucket: a range of lift values where drag stays stubbornly low, then climbs steeply at the ends. You want to operate inside that bucket.

4L/D peaks before stall — not at it

Here's the trap that catches beginners. Your instinct says “more angle → more lift → deeper.” But efficiency — L/D, the number that sets your depth — peaks at a fairly modest angle, well below stall. Crank the angle toward CL,max and you do get more lift, but drag grows faster, so L/D falls. On the polar, peak L/D is the point a straight line from the origin just kisses the curve.

5Explore the curves

Switch between the three views, drag the angle, and swap sections. Watch the operating dot. Find each section's peak L/D (the green star in the L/D view) and notice how far it sits below the stall band. Then flip to the polar and see the same point as the origin-tangent. Turn on overlay to compare the flat plate against the cambered NACA 4412.

The coefficient models here are simple analytic fits — qualitatively right, not a real polar. Getting honest numbers for your shape, at your Reynolds number, is exactly what XFLR5 and CFD are for (Phase 2).

★ The one win from this lesson

A foil is its CL and CD curves. CL rises ~linearly with angle then stalls; the polar shows a low-drag bucket; and peak L/D sits at a modest angle, below stall. Design for peak L/D, not for maximum lift.

⚠ The catch — these curves move

A polar is only valid for one Reynolds number. The same foil's curves shift as speed and size change — and at the low Reynolds numbers our small, slow depressors live in, drag is higher and CL,max lower than the pretty textbook polars promise. That's Lesson 7. First, two things that shape these curves: the section (Lesson 5) and the aspect ratio (Lesson 6).

What you can now say

  1. A foil's character lives in CL and CD — dimensionless shape-and-angle numbers.
  2. CL rises ~linearly with angle (~0.1/°), then stalls; camber shifts the zero-lift angle.
  3. The polar (CL vs CD) shows the low-drag bucket.
  4. Peak L/D — and so deepest towing — sits at a modest angle, below stall.
  5. Polars are Reynolds-number specific (Lesson 7).
