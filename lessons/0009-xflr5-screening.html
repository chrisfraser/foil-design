XFLR5: Screening Sections, Fast and Free

Before you spend hours on 3-D CFD, spend seconds ranking candidate sections — the right first tool gives you real polars at your Reynolds number for the cost of a few clicks.

⏱ ~15 minutes• 🧮 Software workflow, no maths• Your cheap, fast first filter
◎ Why this lesson

Lesson 8 explained what CFD is and why 3-D runs cost real time and patience. But you don't want to spend that effort on six sections you haven't shortlisted. XFLR5 is free software that computes a section's polars in seconds — the perfect tool to ask “which of these candidates makes the best L/D at my conditions?” and throw away the losers before you ever open a 3-D solver.

1What XFLR5 is — and is for

XFLR5 wraps XFOIL, a fast, well-proven 2-D panel + boundary-layer method, in a friendly interface.1 You hand it a foil section and a Reynolds number; in return it computes, at your conditions:

  • The CL–α curve — lift versus angle, including the stall and CL,max (Lesson 4).
  • The drag polar — CL vs CD, with its low-drag bucket.
  • Cm (the pitching moment) and the transition points where the boundary layer trips from laminar to turbulent.

That is exactly the data you learned to read in Lesson 4 — now computed for the specific shapes you are choosing between. It is rapid 2-D section analysis, and that is what it is brilliant at.

2The workflow, start to shortlist

Five steps take you from “I have some candidate sections” to “these one or two are worth real CFD.”

  1. Get or define the section. Use the built-in NACA generator (type 4412 and it builds the coordinates) or import a .dat coordinate file for a section you found elsewhere. This is your “Foil” in XFLR5's Direct Foil Design view.
  2. Set the Reynolds number for YOUR foil. This is the step beginners skip — and then they wonder why the numbers look nothing like reality. Use the figure from Lesson 7: for a 100 mm chord at 5 kn that's roughly Re ≈ 2.6×10⁵. A polar is only valid at one Reynolds number, so this single field decides whether your whole analysis is meaningful.2
  3. Run a batch (Type 1) analysis. A Type 1 analysis holds Reynolds number fixed and sweeps angle of attack across your range — say −4° to +14° in 0.5° steps. Run it and XFLR5 fills in the whole polar in seconds.
  4. Read the polars. Find the drag bucket, the CL,max / stall point, and — the one that sets your dive depth — the peak-L/D angle (Lesson 4). Flip to the L/D-vs-α view to read the peak straight off.
  5. Rank and shortlist. Compare candidates by peak L/D within the CL band you actually need for your downforce — not in the abstract. Keep the best one or two; those are what graduate to 3-D CFD next lesson.

3Screen the candidates

Overlaying polars to rank sections is exactly what XFLR5 produces. The widget below stands in for that: swap sections, turn on compare, and read the L/D-vs-α view — whichever curve peaks highest within your operating band wins the screen. Notice the peak sits at a modest angle, comfortably below stall (Lesson 4).

This widget is a stand-in for the real thing — its curves are simple analytic fits. XFLR5 gives you honest numbers at the exact Reynolds number you type in. The shape of the comparison — overlaid polars you rank by peak L/D — is what matters here.

4What a screen looks like

The output of a screening session is a short ranked table. Below is an illustrative example (not measured numbers) of how you'd record three candidates at your Reynolds number — enough to pick a winner and drop the rest.

Section~Peak L/DStall αNotes
Flat plate~12~9°Easy to make; modest efficiency, early stall.
NACA 0012 (symmetric)~40~14°Predictable; no camber, so works the same flipped.
NACA 4412 (cambered)~55~13°Best L/D here; wider drag bucket. Shortlist this.

Illustrative only — real values depend on your Reynolds number and will be lower than textbook figures at the small, slow scale depressors live in (Lesson 7). Trust the ranking, not the exact magnitudes.

5The line you must not cross

Here is the rule that keeps this whole course honest. XFLR5's 2-D section results are trustworthy and exactly what you want for screening. But XFLR5 can also predict 3-D wing behaviour using lifting-line / vortex-lattice methods — and those are unreliable for low aspect ratio.3 Depressors are stubby, low-AR shapes (Lesson 6), so XFLR5's 3-D lift and drag numbers for one are not to be believed.

So draw the line clearly: use XFLR5 to pick a SECTION, never to get the final 3-D lift/drag of your depressor. The honest 3-D answer comes from real 3-D CFD — SimScale, next lesson. And even for the 2-D screen, remember XFLR5 can be optimistic at low Reynolds number: treat absolute numbers with caution and trust the relative ranking more than the magnitudes.

★ The one win from this lesson

XFLR5 gives fast, free 2-D section polars at your Reynolds number — ideal for screening and ranking candidate sections by peak L/D in your operating band. But distrust its low-AR 3-D numbers: use it to choose a section, not to size a depressor.
