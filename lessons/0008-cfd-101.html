CFD 101: What a Simulation Actually Computes

Before you click a single button in XFLR5 or SimScale, understand what's happening under the hood — so a confident-looking number doesn't fool you.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🧮 Concepts, no heavy math• Concepts before you click
◎ Why this lesson

Phase 1 gave you the physics: lift, drag, the lift equation, polars, Reynolds number. Now we hand those same equations to a computer. CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) will spit out pretty pictures and confident numbers for CL, CD and L/D — but only some of them are trustworthy. This lesson is the map of what the software is actually doing, so the buttons mean something and you can smell a bad result before it sends you cutting the wrong foil.

1The big idea: chop the water up and solve the physics in each piece

CFD does something almost embarrassingly literal. It takes the water around your foil, chops it into millions of tiny cells (a mesh), and in every single cell it numerically solves the governing equations of fluid motion — the Navier–Stokes equations, which are just conservation of mass and conservation of momentum written for a fluid.1 Solve for the velocity and pressure in every cell, then add up the pressure and shear over the foil's surface, and you get the total force — split it into lift and drag, divide out ½ρv²A, and you have CL and CD.

That's it. It is the same physics from Lessons 1–4, solved arithmetically a few million times instead of with one tidy equation. The computer isn't smarter than the lift equation — it's just able to handle the messy, curved, separating real flow that no clean formula can.

2Five concepts to know before you click anything

Every CFD tool — XFLR5, SimScale, OpenFOAM — wraps the same five decisions in different buttons. Get these and the interface stops being mysterious.

  1. Mesh — how finely you chop the water. Resolution matters. Too coarse and you miss the thin boundary layer where drag is born; too fine and the run takes hours. The craft is refining cells near the surface and leaving the farfield coarse.
  2. Boundary conditions — what you pin at the edges: inlet velocity (your troll speed), outlet pressure, a no-slip wall on the foil (water sticks to the surface), and symmetry / farfield on the box. Garbage boundary conditions → garbage answer, no matter how nice the mesh.
  3. Turbulence model — real flow is turbulent, and resolving every eddy is far too expensive, so most hobby CFD uses RANS with a model like k-ω SST — a solid default for foils with mild separation.2 It is an approximation of turbulence, not the truth.
  4. y+ — a dimensionless measure of how close your first cell sits to the wall. You target y+ ≈ 1 to resolve the boundary layer directly (or use wall functions and aim higher). Wrong y+ → wrong wall shear → wrong drag.3
  5. Convergence — the solver iterates toward an answer. You watch the residuals fall and the lift/drag settle onto a plateau. An un-converged run is meaningless, however pretty its contour plot.

3Convergence: only trust a number once it stops moving

That last one deserves a picture, because it's the single most common rookie mistake — reading a force off a run that was still wandering. The solver doesn't get the answer in one shot; it sweeps over the mesh again and again, each pass nudging the solution closer to one that satisfies Navier–Stokes everywhere. Two things should flatten out: the residuals (how badly the equations are still violated) and the quantity you care about (lift or drag). When both stop changing, the run has converged.

4The honest warning: pretty is not correct

Here is the spine of this whole course. CFD produces confident-looking numbers and gorgeous colour plots that can be flat-out wrong — and it gives you no warning when they are. A bad mesh, the wrong fluid properties, an inappropriate turbulence model, the wrong y+, or a run you stopped before it converged will each hand you a clean, plausible, completely false L/D.

So the rule is non-negotiable: every CFD result gets sanity-checked against a hand calculation. Does the CL roughly match the lift equation (Lesson 2) at this angle? Is the L/D in the ballpark you'd expect from a polar (Lesson 4)? Is it even the right Reynolds number regime (Lesson 7)? If the sim says L/D = 80 for a flat plate at low Reynolds number, the sim is wrong — not physics. Pretty ≠ correct.

◇ Your three tools, in one line each

This phase uses three: XFLR5 for fast 2-D section screening (next lesson), SimScale for approachable browser-based 3-D CFD, and OpenFOAM for deep, no-limits validation. Which to reach for, and their trade-offs, live on the CFD tools comparison page.

★ The one win from this lesson

CFD = mesh the water + solve Navier–Stokes in every cell + integrate the surface forces → CL/CD. Trust the answer only after you've checked the mesh, boundary conditions, turbulence model, y+ and convergence — and sanity-checked it against a hand calculation. The same physics from Phase 1, solved a million times over.
