Phase 2 · CAD & CFD · Lesson 10

SimScale Hands-On

Push your Fusion 360 depressor into real 3-D water CFD — in a browser tab, for free — and finally get honest lift and drag for the actual shape you drew.

⏱ ~16 minutes• 🖱 A click-path, not a calculation• From CAD model to real CFD
◎ Why this lesson

Lesson 9 used XFLR5 to screen sections fast — but it's 2-D, and it can't be trusted on the short, stubby, low-aspect-ratio shape a real depressor actually is. The tip vortices and induced drag from Lesson 6 live in the third dimension, and 2-D screening pretends they don't exist. SimScale is the easiest step up: browser-based 3-D CFD that takes your Fusion 360 part and tows it through real water. This lesson is the click-path — and the four ways beginners get a confident wrong answer.

1Why SimScale for your first real 3-D run

You could install OpenFOAM and learn Linux, or buy a seat of a commercial solver. Not yet. SimScale wins for the first honest run because it is free on the community tier, runs entirely in the browser (nothing to install, the solve happens on their cloud), and has a clean Fusion 360 → CFD path.1 Most importantly it does genuine 3-D: it sees the wing tips, the spanwise flow, and the induced drag that XFLR5's 2-D strip theory can only guess at. For a low-AR depressor that difference is the whole ball game.

2The shape of the workflow

Before the clicks, hold the pipeline in your head. The thing you actually simulate is not the foil — it's the water around it, with the foil-shaped hole subtracted out. Everything downstream depends on getting that, the fluid, and the mesh right.

3The click-path, step by step

Have your foil designed in Fusion and a trolling speed in mind (convert knots to m/s — 1 knot ≈ 0.514 m/s). Then, inside a SimScale project:

  1. Geometry — export & enclose. Export the foil from Fusion 360 as a STEP file and upload it. Then build a flow domain: a box of fluid around the foil, big enough that the walls don't interfere — several chord-lengths to each side and above/below, and more downstream (the wake needs room). SimScale's "external flow domain" / enclosure tool does this and subtracts the foil, so what you mesh is the water with a foil-shaped hole in it.
  2. Mesh — coarse first, with a boundary layer. Use the automatic mesher and add boundary-layer (inflation) refinement on the foil surface, because the lift and drag are decided in that thin sheared layer against the wall — recall y+ from Lesson 8. Start coarse to shake out setup mistakes cheaply; only refine once the case runs end to end.
  3. Analysis type — Incompressible. Water doesn't compress at trolling speeds, so choose the incompressible fluid-flow analysis. (Compressible solvers are for things going near the speed of sound — not us.)
  4. Materials — set the fluid to WATER. This is the number-one beginner mistake: SimScale defaults to air. Change it to water — density ρ ≈ 1000 kg/m³ (fresh) or 1025 (sea) and water's dynamic viscosity (≈ 0.001 Pa·s). Leave it on air and water is ~800× denser, so every force comes out roughly 800× too small and you won't notice until it's far too late.
  5. Boundary conditions — tell the box what's happening. Set the inlet to your trolling speed in m/s; the outlet to a pressure (gauge 0); the foil surface to a no-slip wall; and the box sides/top/bottom to slip or symmetry so the fake walls don't drag on the flow.
  6. Turbulence model — k-ω SST. It's the reliable default for foils: it behaves well both in the near-wall boundary layer and out in the free stream, which is exactly the mix a towed foil produces.
  7. Run, then watch it converge. Launch the simulation on the cloud and watch the residuals and the force plots settle (convergence, from Lesson 8). A run that's still wandering is not an answer.
  8. Post-process — read the forces, then convert. Pull the lift and drag forces on the foil surface. Back them out into CL, CD and L/D with the lift equation, and compare to the hand estimate you made back in Lesson 2 / Lesson 4.
⚠ The four ways to get a confident wrong answer

(1) Wrong fluid — left on air, forces ~800× off. (2) Privacy — the free community tier makes projects public; don't upload anything you need to keep secret. (3) Not actually converged or mesh-independent — a number that's still drifting, or that changes when you refine the mesh, isn't a result. (4) No sanity check — a tidy, converged CFD number that disagrees 3× with your hand calc means something is wrong, almost always the fluid, the units, or the mesh. CFD doesn't replace the back-of-envelope check — it earns trust by agreeing with it.

★ The one win from this lesson

SimScale turns a Fusion 360 foil into real 3-D water CFD for honest lift and drag — provided you set the fluid to water, mesh the boundary layer, run incompressible + k-ω SST, reach convergence, and sanity-check against a hand calc. It's the first time you get numbers for your actual low-AR shape that include the tip losses XFLR5 can't see.

What you can now say

  1. SimScale is free, browser-based 3-D CFD with a clean Fusion 360 → water path.
  2. You simulate the flow domain — a generous box of water with the foil subtracted.
  3. Mesh with a boundary layer (y+), run incompressible + k-ω SST.
  4. Set the fluid to water (ρ ≈ 1000–1025) — the default air is off by ~800×.
  5. Believe a number only after convergence, mesh-independence, and a hand-calc sanity check.
