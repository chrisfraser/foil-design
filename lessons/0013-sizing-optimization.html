Sizing & Optimization

Every thread of this course finally ties into one real part: a foil sized for your speed, your depth, and your tackle.

⏱ ~15 minutes• 🧮 Design backwards from requirements• The capstone — turning theory into a buildable foil
◎ Why this lesson

You've collected all the pieces: the lift equation (Lesson 2), L/D and polars (Lessons 4–5), aspect ratio (Lesson 6), Reynolds number (Lesson 7), CFD-measured coefficients (Phase 2), and the dive-depth force balance (Lesson 3). This is where they stop being separate facts and become one design. The trick is to stop thinking about parts and start from requirements — then let the equations hand you the foil.

1Start from requirements, not parts

The beginner mistake is to start with a shape — “I'll make a NACA something at 10°” — and ask what it does. The designer does the opposite: writes down what the job demands, then solves for the foil. Four numbers define the job:

  • Trolling speed v — the speed you actually fish at, somewhere in your 3–10 kn band. Everything scales with ½ρv², so commit to a number.
  • Target depth — how deep you want the lure to track, in metres.
  • Downforce L — enough to track deep and steady against your lure/tackle drag, but not so much it rips the rod down or buries the gear. Match it to your terminal tackle.
  • Tow-load limit — the most your line, rod, cleat or downrigger can safely hold. This is a hard ceiling, and at speed it's the number that breaks gear, not the downforce.

Write those four down first. Design backwards from them and the foil almost falls out of the maths.

2The design chain, step by step

Here's the whole course as one procedure. Each step leans on a lesson you've already done:

  1. Pick a section. Choose a foil section (Lesson 5). That gives you an operating CL and a CD, hence a 2-D L/D you can screen quickly in XFLR5 at your Reynolds number (Lessons 7/9).1
  2. Choose the angle of attack. Read the polar (Lesson 4) and sit at the angle for peak L/D, comfortably below stall. That fixes CL and CD.
  3. Size the area from the lift equation. Solve L = ½ρv²·A·CL for area (see the formula below). Bigger area = more downforce — but also more drag, more tow load, and a bigger part to print.
  4. Set the aspect ratio. Higher AR (Lesson 6) cuts induced drag and lifts real L/D — but a long thin foil is weaker and harder to print. Depressors compromise low. Remember the 3-D L/D is worse than the 2-D screening value because of induced drag.
  5. Verify in CFD. Run the 3-D shape in SimScale/OpenFOAM (Phase 2) for the real coefficients — and trust the number only after it passes Lesson 11's gates (converged, mesh-independent, validated).
  6. Predict the depth. Feed the verified L/D and your line length into the dive geometry (Lesson 3): θ = arctan(L/D), depth = ℓ·sin θ — then knock it down by your real-world factor η for line drag and weight.

3Sizing the area — the key equation

Step 3 is the heart of it. Take the lift equation from Lesson 2 and turn it inside out: you know the downforce you want, so solve for the area that delivers it.

A = downforce / (½ · ρ · v² · CL) ρ = water density (≈1025 salt, 1000 fresh) · v = speed (m/s) · CL = lift coeff. from step 2  |  result in m²

Read it as a balancing act. A higher CL (more camber or more angle) lets you hit the same downforce with a smaller foil — but it costs L/D, brings stall closer, and raises cavitation risk (Lesson 12). A bigger area is gentler on CL but adds drag and tow load and uses more filament. There is no single best — there's the best for your four numbers.

4It's a balancing act, not a single answer

Every knob trades against another:

  • More area → more downforce, but more drag and a higher tow load.
  • More camber / higher CL → more lift per area, but lower L/D, earlier stall, and more cavitation risk.
  • Higher aspect ratio → better L/D, but a weaker part that's harder to print and tow.

Because the optimum depends on your speed, depth, tackle and printer, the smart move isn't to guess once — it's a mini design-of-experiments. Sketch 2–3 candidate designs, run each through CFD, and pick the winner against your requirements. Two or three honest 3-D runs beat a month of arguing on paper.

5Design it — the capstone calculator

This is the course's capstone tool. It chains everything: the lift equation for downforce, the drag equation for tow load, L/D, and the dive geometry for the depth you'll actually run. Dial in your speed and your target depth, then hunt for an area + coefficients combination that delivers both — while keeping the tow load under your gear's limit.

A worked target to try: “about 7 kgf of downforce and roughly 25 m of depth at 5 kn.” Start near CL 0.9 / CD 0.13, then trade area against CL until the cards line up — and watch the tow load while you do it.

★ The one win from this lesson

Design backwards from requirements. Fix your four numbers — speed, target depth, downforce, tow-load limit — then: pick a section and angle for peak L/D below stall, size the area from A = downforce / (½ρv²·CL), compromise aspect ratio for strength, verify the 3-D numbers in CFD, and predict depth via Lesson 3. It's a balance tuned to your conditions, not a single right answer — so run 2–3 candidates and pick the winner.

A design on screen is only a hypothesis. Every number here — even a CFD result that passed all three gates — is a prediction until water votes on it. The only verdict that counts is the tow: print it, run it, measure the real depth, and calibrate your η. That's the finale — Lesson 14.
