Foil Sections: Flat Plate vs Cambered

The 2-D cross-section you choose is the curves you met last lesson. A little camber and the right thickness move the whole polar — for free.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 Graphs, no calculus• Where the curves come from
◎ Why this lesson

Lesson 4 taught you to read a foil's CL and CD curves — the slope, the stall, the drag bucket, the peak-L/D point. But what decides those curves? The section: the 2-D shape of the foil's cross-section. Change the section and you redraw every curve. This lesson is how a few shape choices — camber and thickness — move the polar up, left, and deeper, and how to read the NACA name stamped on a section so you can pick a good one for a printed depressor instead of settling for a flat plate.

1The section is the cross-section — and it sets the curves

Slice a foil straight through, perpendicular to its span, and the outline you see is the section (or profile). It's a 2-D shape, defined by two things laid over the chord line: the mean camber line (how the shape curves) and the thickness (how fat it is). Those two choices are what actually produce the CL–α slope, the CL,max, and the drag bucket you learned to read.1 Pick the section and you've picked the curves.

2The flat plate: honest, printable, mediocre

The simplest section is a flat plate — no camber, barely any thickness. It does make lift (any angled surface does), it prints in minutes, and it's a perfectly good way to prove the idea works. But it pays for that simplicity: a sharp leading edge means the flow trips and separates early, so a flat plate stalls at a low angle and carries more drag for the lift it makes. Fine for a first wet test; not what you want bolted to your line for a season. It's the baseline every better section beats.

3Camber: lift you get almost for free

Now curve the mean line — give the section camber, like a gentle banana. This is the single biggest "free" win in section choice. A cambered section:

  • makes lift at zero angle of attack. A symmetric section makes CL = 0 at α = 0°; a cambered one is already working. Its whole CL–α line is shifted left, so its zero-lift angle is negative — roughly α0 ≈ −4° for a NACA 4412.2
  • reaches a higher CL,max for less drag — a better, deeper drag bucket sitting further up and to the left on the polar. More lift, lower drag, at the same time.

You can read that zero-lift shift straight off the lift line. With a slope near 0.1 per degree, the lift at α = 0° is just the slope times the offset from α0:

CL (α=0) ≈ 0.1 · (0 − α0) = 0.1 · 4° = 0.4 a NACA 4412 already makes a healthy CL ≈ 0.4 at zero geometric angle — a flat plate makes zero

One twist for us: a depressor points its lift down. So we orient the camber belly-up — the mirror image of an aircraft wing. Same shape, same free lift, just flipped so the perpendicular force aims at the lake-bed.

4Thickness: strength and a gentler stall

The other dial is thickness. Fattening the section costs a little drag, but it buys two things that matter a lot for a real, towed, printed part:

  • Structural strength. You're 3D-printing this and towing it at up to ~10 knots — the bending loads are real. Thickness is depth in a beam, and beam stiffness rises fast with depth. A thicker section simply doesn't snap.
  • A gentler, more forgiving stall. A rounded, thicker leading edge lets the flow stay attached longer and lets stall arrive softly instead of as a cliff — handy when your angle of attack wanders in real water.

Typical foils run about 10–15% thick (thickness as a fraction of chord). For a printed depressor that band is your friend: enough material to be strong and to give the trailing edge something to actually print, without throwing away much L/D.

5Decoding the NACA 4-digit name

Classic sections come with a 4-digit NACA name that is its geometry. Read it digit by digit:

NACA 4412 4 → max camber = 4% of chord  ·  4 → camber sits at 40% of chord back from the nose  ·  12 → max thickness = 12% of chord

So NACA 4412 is a moderately cambered, moderately thick, well-behaved section — a sensible starting point. A name beginning 00 means zero camber: a symmetric section. NACA 0012 is the symmetric cousin — same 12% thickness, no camber, so CL = 0 at α = 0° and a polar centred on the origin. Compare 4412 against 0012 and you're seeing exactly what camber alone does.

6Explore the curves

Turn on overlay and put all three up together — the flat plate, the symmetric NACA 0012, and the cambered NACA 4412. Watch what camber does: it shifts the whole polar up and to the left — more lift, lower drag — and pushes the lift line left so the cambered section already makes lift at α = 0°. Notice the flat plate stalls earliest and runs widest on drag. Then flip to the polar view and find each section's deeper, better-placed drag bucket.

These are simple analytic fits — qualitatively right, not measured polars. For honest numbers on your section at your Reynolds number, that's what XFLR5 and CFD are for (Phase 2).

7So what should our depressor use?

A moderately cambered, moderately thick NACA-style section — something in the spirit of a 4412 — beats a flat plate on L/D while staying strong and printable. Camber buys lift cheaply; thickness buys strength and a kinder stall. But none of it is truly free, and the trade-offs all point the same way if you overdo it:

  • More camber → more lift, but drag and the nose-down pitching moment grow too (the foil tries harder to twist — that loads your mount).
  • More thickness → stronger and softer-stalling, but draggier.
  • Very thin trailing edges are hard to print cleanly and fragile once printed — another reason a sensible thickness wins for us.

Moderate, not maximal, is the printed-depressor sweet spot.

★ The one win from this lesson

The section sets the curves. Camber buys lift cheaply — lift at zero angle and a higher CL,max for less drag — and thickness buys strength and a gentler stall. Pick a moderate cambered NACA section over a flat plate, oriented belly-up for a depressor.

⚠ Next thread to pull

Everything here is a 2-D ideal — a section, as if the foil ran on forever (infinite span). A real depressor is short and stubby, and it has tips. At the tips the pressure leaks around and bleeds off lift while adding a brand-new kind of drag. Short foils lose a lot there. That's aspect ratio and induced drag — Lesson 6.

What you can now say

  1. The section (2-D cross-section: mean line + thickness) is what sets the CL/CD curves.
  2. A flat plate works but stalls early and drags more — a baseline, not an optimum.
  3. Camber buys lift cheaply: lift at α = 0° (negative zero-lift angle, ≈ −4° for 4412) and a deeper bucket.
  4. Thickness (~10–15%) buys strength and a gentler stall for a little drag — ideal for a printed part.
  5. NACA 4412 = 4% camber, at 40% chord, 12% thick; 00xx = symmetric.
  6. For us: a moderate cambered NACA section beats a flat plate, oriented belly-up.
