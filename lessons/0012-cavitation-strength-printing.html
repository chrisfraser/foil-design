Cavitation, Strength & Printing

Two ways a perfectly-designed foil betrays you in the real water: the flow boils, or the part snaps. Here's how to see both coming before you tow.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 One formula, plenty of intuition• Where physics meets the print bed
◎ Why this lesson

In Lesson 11 you learned to trust a number. Now you're committing to a design and making it real — and reality has two failure modes the lift equation never mentioned. First the flow itself can break: push hard enough and the water on your foil literally boils, a wreck called cavitation. Second the part can break: a 3D-printed foil towed at 10 knots carries a real, growing load, and FDM plastic has a grain you have to respect. This lesson is both — when each one bites, and how to stay clear.

1Cavitation: when the water boils on your foil

You already know a foil makes lift by running a low-pressure region over its suction side. Drop that pressure far enough and something strange happens: water doesn't need to be hot to boil — it boils when the local pressure falls below its vapour pressure (a tiny ≈ 2.3 kPa for cool water). The flow flashes into vapour bubbles, which sweep downstream into higher pressure and collapse violently. That collapse wrecks your lift, shakes the rig with vibration and noise, and — over time — hammers tiny pits into the surface. 1 It is the foil's equivalent of the flow giving up, except instead of gently separating it detonates.

2The cavitation number σ: one ratio that predicts it

Whether you cavitate comes down to a tug-of-war: the ambient pressure trying to keep water liquid, versus the dynamic pressure (½ρv²) the flow uses to carve out low-pressure zones. Bottle that ratio into the cavitation number, σ:

σ = (patm + ρ · g · d − pv) / (½ · ρ · v²) patm atmospheric pressure (≈ 101 kPa)  ·  ρ·g·d water pressure at depth d  ·  pv vapour pressure (≈ 2.3 kPa)  ·  ½ρv² dynamic pressure q

Read the top as "how much pressure is holding the water together" and the bottom as "how hard the flow is pulling it apart." The foil cavitates when its suction peak — the deepest dip in its pressure coefficient, |Cp,min| — exceeds σ. A gently-loaded foil might peak at |Cp,min| ≈ 1; a hard-working, heavily-cambered one at 3 or more. Compare that number to σ and you have your answer.

3The counter-intuitive levers

This is the part worth tattooing on the inside of your eyelids, because two of the three levers run backwards from intuition:

  • Deeper is safer. Every metre down adds ρ·g·d to the numerator, so σ rises and the extra ambient pressure squashes the bubbles before they form. Going deep protects you.
  • Faster is riskier. Speed sits in the denominator as v², so σ falls fast as you speed up — double the speed and the dynamic pressure quadruples. The top of your speed range is the danger zone.
  • A harder-working foil cavitates sooner. More camber or a higher CL deepens the suction peak (bigger |Cp,min|), so it punches through σ earlier. Working the foil hard for lift costs you cavitation margin.

Play with the calculator. Start at the default — it's safe. Then push SPEED up and DEPTH down and watch σ fall toward the threshold; finally raise the suction peak |Cp,min| to model a hard-working foil and tip it into cavitation. The verdict and margin card tell you how much room you have.

A first-order σ model: it captures the fast/shallow/high-load trade-off cleanly, but real onset also depends on surface finish, the exact pressure distribution and Reynolds number.

4Honest reality: for us, it's an edge case

Don't let the drama scare you off. At trolling speeds of 3–10 knots, cavitation is usually a minor risk for a sensibly-designed depressor. It only bites in one specific corner: fast, shallow, and highly loaded — exactly the corner of the calculator where σ collapses. The worst case is a heavily-cambered foil run flat-out near the surface at 10 knots. Tow deeper, ease off the camber, or back off the throttle and the problem evaporates. Treat it as a corner to respect, not a constant threat. One caveat: a rough print or a nicked leading edge trips it earlier — a low-pressure dimple at a surface flaw seeds the first bubble. Which is a perfect hand-off to the second half of this lesson: finish quality.

5The load your part actually carries

Back in Lesson 2 you saw the tow load can be tens of kilograms-force — and crucially it scales with v². The part that feels solid at 5 knots sees roughly 4× that load at 10 knots. Your foil is a little cantilever beam: held at one end (the tow attachment / root) with the water pressing along its span. Bending stress is highest right at the root, where the leverage is greatest. Here a piece of earlier geometry pays off — the low-aspect-ratio, stubby planform from Lesson 6 has a short moment arm, so the same load makes less bending stress than a long slender wing would. Stubby is strong.

6Print orientation is (almost) everything

Here's the single biggest mistake people make printing a structural part: ignoring the grain. FDM prints are weakest between layers — the bond across layer lines is far weaker than the plastic within a layer, so parts delaminate by peeling apart along those lines. 2 The rule: orient the part so the main bending load runs along the layers, not across them.

Concretely: don't print a thin foil lying flat on the bed, because then the root-bending load pulls directly on the weakest interlayer bond and peels it open. Print it on edge (span roughly vertical) so the layers stack across the chord and the bending stress runs through solid material, or otherwise choose the orientation that puts the strongest grain at the root.

7Material, infill & finish

For a part you tow in (often salt) water and leave in the sun, skip brittle PLA. Reach for:

  • PETG or ASA/ABS — tough, water- and UV-tolerant, far less likely to shatter on a snag than PLA. For the serious version, nylon or fibre-filled filament.
  • Infill where it counts — high infill or solid near the root and the leading edge (the loaded, impact-prone regions); the trailing edge can run thinner to save weight and print time.
  • Smooth the surface — sand and/or coat it. Roughness both adds drag and seeds cavitation, so finish is doing double duty.

8A practical build checklist

Pulling the structural rules together into the order you'll actually use them:

  1. Thicken the sectionA fuller section (from Lesson 5) buys both bending strength and printability — thin trailing edges are fragile and hard to print well.
  2. Fillet the rootAdd a generous fillet where the foil meets the strut/tow point; sharp inside corners are stress concentrators that crack first.
  3. Embed the tow pointConsider a metal rod or bolt moulded into the print to carry the tow load instead of trusting plastic threads.
  4. Orient for the grainPrint on edge so layers run across the chord and the root-bending load stays in solid material, not across layer lines.
  5. Seal & smoothCoat against water ingress and sand the surface — protects the part and removes cavitation-seeding roughness.
★ The one win from this lesson

Cavitation is a fast-shallow-high-load edge risk set by σ = (patm + ρgd − pv) / ½ρv² — deeper = safer, faster = riskier, and it bites when |Cp,min| > σ. And a printed foil must survive a v²-scaling tow load, so choose a tough material, orient the layers across the bending load, beef up the root, and finish the surface smooth.
