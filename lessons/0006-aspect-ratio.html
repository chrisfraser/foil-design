Aspect Ratio & Induced Drag

Your foil has ends — and at those ends, lift leaks away as a whole new kind of drag. For short, stubby depressors, this is often the drag that matters most.

⏱ ~13 minutes• 🧮 One formula, plenty of intuition• The price of having tips
◎ Why this lesson

The polars in Lesson 4 and the sections in Lesson 5 quietly assumed a foil of infinite span — a pure 2-D slice with no ends. Real foils have tips, and tips change everything. At the tips, the flow leaks around the edge and spills your lift into a brand-new drag — induced drag. For the short, stubby foils a depressor wants, this leakage is brutal, and it's controlled by one number you get to choose: aspect ratio.

1Aspect ratio: long-and-thin vs short-and-stubby

The aspect ratio measures how slender a foil is — how much span it has relative to its chord:

AR = b² / A = b / c AR aspect ratio  ·  b span (tip-to-tip)  ·  A planform area  ·  c chord (the right-hand form holds for a rectangle)

A glider wing is high-AR — long, thin, elegant (AR of 15–30). A depressor is usually low-AR — short, stubby, compact and strong (AR of roughly 1–4). Same idea, opposite ends of the scale. And that choice, it turns out, is one of the biggest levers you have over efficiency.

2Why the tips cost you

Your foil makes its perpendicular force by holding higher pressure on one face and lower pressure on the other. In the middle of a long span, that pressure difference just sits there doing its job. But at an open tip, the two faces meet — and high pressure does what high pressure always does: it leaks around the edge toward the low-pressure side.

That leaking flow rolls up into a pair of swirling tip vortices trailing off the ends.1 They cost you twice over:

  • Near the tips your foil can no longer hold its full pressure difference, so it spills lift — the ends are working at a discount.
  • The trailing vortices tilt the local flow downward (a downwash), which tips your lift vector slightly backward. Part of what should have been pure lift now points along the flow as drag.

3Induced drag: the price of making lift

That second effect is induced drag — sometimes called drag-due-to-lift, because unlike the friction-and-shape drag of Lesson 4, it exists only because the foil is making lift with finite ends. It has a clean formula:2

CDi = CL² / (π · AR · e) CDi induced-drag coefficient  ·  CL lift coefficient  ·  AR aspect ratio  ·  e Oswald efficiency factor (≈ 0.8)

Read it as two warnings. First, induced drag grows with CL squared — so it bites hardest exactly when you're working the foil hard for downforce. Second, it shrinks with AR — so halving the aspect ratio roughly doubles the induced drag. The e term (the Oswald efficiency factor, usually ≈ 0.8) just docks you a little for not having a perfectly elliptical lift shape.

4The depressor's double bind

Now stack the depressor's reality on top of that formula. A depressor runs low AR (stubby and strong) and high CL (you want lots of downforce). Both terms in CDi = CL²/(π·AR·e) are pushed the wrong way at once. The result: for many depressors, induced drag is the dominant drag — bigger than all the profile drag the section was so carefully shaped to minimise.

And induced drag is the main thing capping your total L/D — which, from Lesson 3, is what sets how deep you dive. So aspect ratio is a top-tier design lever: more span buys you better L/D and more depth. The catch is structural — a longer, more slender foil carries far more bending load at its root, is weaker and floppier, and is harder to print and stow. The whole game is trading span for strength.

5The 2-D polars were the best case

Here's the tie-back that matters for the rest of the course. The pretty polars from Lessons 4–5 — and the ones XFLR5 will hand you later — are 2-D, infinite-span numbers. They are the AR → ∞ ideal. A real, finite, low-AR depressor is always worse: same section, same angle, but now paying induced drag on top. This is exactly why you must distrust a tool's optimistic numbers for a stubby foil, and why Phase 2 is about verifying them. The shape's character is real; its finite-foil tax is the part theory keeps trying to hide from you.

6Explore the trade

Drag the aspect ratio down toward 1–3 — typical depressor territory — and watch the planform go stubby at constant area, the tip vortices fatten, and the induced slice of the drag bar swallow the profile slice. Track the readouts: CDi climbing, the total CD following it, and your L/D at this AR cratering far below the L/D if AR → ∞ ideal. That gap is the price of having tips.

A teaching model using CDi = CL²/(π·AR·e) — qualitatively right, not a substitute for solving your planform. Getting an honest finite-foil L/D is what XFLR5 and CFD are for (Phase 2).

★ The one win from this lesson

Real foils leak lift at the tips, which costs you induced drag, CDi = CL²/(π·AR·e). Low-AR depressors run high CL and low AR, so they pay this heavily — which makes aspect ratio one of your biggest levers on L/D and dive depth, traded off against strength.

What you can now say

  1. Aspect ratio AR = b²/A (= span/chord for a rectangle) measures slenderness; depressors are low-AR.
  2. Open tips leak flow into tip vortices that spill lift and tilt it into drag.
  3. Induced drag: CDi = CL²/(π·AR·e) — grows with CL², shrinks with AR.
  4. Low AR + high CL makes induced drag dominant for depressors, capping L/D and depth.
  5. 2-D polars are the AR → ∞ ideal; a real finite foil is always worse — so verify (Phase 2).
