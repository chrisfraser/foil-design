What Sets the Dive Depth

Your depressor doesn't sink — it flies down on a leash, and stops where the forces balance.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🧮 Trigonometry of one triangle• Turns force into depth
◎ Why this lesson

In Lesson 2 you computed the downforce a foil makes. But downforce alone doesn't tell you how deep your lure runs. A depressor isn't a sinker — it's a wing on a string, and it settles at the depth where three forces balance. This lesson turns “6.9 kg of downforce” into “runs at 29 m,” and reveals a result that surprises almost everyone: speed barely changes the depth.

1It's a tug-of-war between three forces

Picture the depressor mid-troll. Three forces act on it:

  • Downforce L — the foil's lift, aimed down (Lesson 2). This drives it deep.
  • Drag D — the toll along the flow, pointing back, away from the boat.
  • Line tension T — the towline, pulling up and forward toward the boat.

(There's also weight and buoyancy, usually small for a thin printed foil — we'll fold those into a correction at the end.) When the depressor stops diving and tracks steadily, these forces are in equilibrium: they cancel. The line tension must exactly oppose the combination of downforce and drag.

△ The force triangle

Downforce points down, drag points back, and the line must pull opposite their sum. So the line can't hang straight down and it can't lie flat — it settles at the one angle where its pull cancels both. That angle is the whole secret to depth.

2The line trails at the L/D angle

Here's the move. For the line to balance the foil, its upward pull must equal the downforce L, and its forward pull must equal the drag D. Divide one by the other and the tension cancels, leaving the angle the line makes below horizontal:

tan θ = L / D θ = angle the towline trails below horizontal · L/D = the foil's lift-to-drag ratio (Lesson 2)

Read that again — it's the punchline of the whole lesson. The towline trails at the angle whose tangent is your foil's L/D. A sleek foil with L/D = 6 pulls the line down to θ ≈ 80°: steep, almost straight down. A draggy flat plate at L/D = 2 only manages θ ≈ 63°. Better foils don't just pull harder — they pull steeper.

3Depth = line length × sin θ

Once you know the angle, depth is pure geometry. The line is the hypotenuse; the depth is the vertical side:

depth = ℓ · sin θ ℓ = length of line let out · combine with θ = arctan(L/D) above

So depth has exactly two knobs: how steep the line trails (your foil's L/D) and how much line you let out (ℓ). Want deeper? Let out more line, or build a higher-L/D foil. That's the lever — and it's why the same depressor can fish 3 m or 50 m just by paying out line.

★ Wait — where did speed go?

Look back: v isn't in either equation. Both L and D grow with ½ρv² (Lesson 2), so when you take the ratio L/D, the v² cancels. Trolling faster makes both forces bigger together — the angle stays put, so the depth stays put. Speed doesn't set depth; it sets tow load. This is why a depressor holds its depth across a range of trolling speeds.

4A worked dive

Take the foil from Lesson 2: at 5 knots it made L ≈ 68 N of downforce. Say its drag is D ≈ 14 N, so L/D ≈ 5. You've let out 30 m of line:

θ = arctan(5) ≈ 79°   →   depth = 30 · sin(79°) ≈ 29 m

Now double the speed to 10 knots. Downforce leaps to ≈ 271 N and drag to ≈ 56 N — both 4× bigger — but L/D is still 5, θ is still 79°, and the depth is still ≈ 29 m. All that the extra speed bought you is a tow load that went from ≈ 70 N to ≈ 280 N. Same depth, four times the strain on your gear.

5Try it — drive the depth

Change the foil's efficiency and the line you pay out, and watch the line angle and depth respond. Notice there's deliberately no speed slider — that's the lesson. Then pull down the “real-world factor” to see how line drag and weight rob you of depth.

The straight-line model is the ceiling. Reality always falls short of it — the next section says why.

6Why reality runs shallower

The clean model assumes a weightless, drag-free line. Two things spoil that, both making you run shallower than ℓ · sin θ:

  • Line drag. The towline is long and moving fast through dense water, so it has its own drag — often a lot. That drag bows the line into a curve and lifts the depressor, sometimes badly. Thin line and short scope help; this is the single biggest real-world loss.1
  • Weight vs buoyancy. A printed foil plus hardware has some net weight (or buoyancy) that nudges the balance. Small next to a punchy foil's downforce, but not zero — and it matters at low speed when forces are weak.

That's what the real-world factor in the tool stands in for. In practice you'll get maybe 50–80% of the ideal depth, and the only way to know your number is to measure it — paint depth marks on the line, or clip on a cheap depth/temperature logger, and calibrate. That measured factor is gold: it's how you'll later check whether a CFD prediction is trustworthy.

★ The one win from this lesson

Depth is a force balance, not a sink rate. The towline trails at tan θ = L/D, and depth = ℓ · sin θ. Two knobs set depth: foil L/D and line let out. Speed cancels — it sets tow load, not depth. Real depth runs shallower (line drag, weight); calibrate the gap by measuring.
